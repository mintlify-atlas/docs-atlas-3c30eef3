## Reading Research Papers Effectively

Research papers are the foundation of cutting-edge AI development. Here's how to read them strategically:

### Three-Pass Approach

<Accordion title="First Pass: 5-10 minutes">
  **Goal:** Understand what the paper is about
  
  **Read:**
  - Title, abstract, introduction
  - Section headings
  - Conclusion
  - Figures and captions
  
  **Decide:**
  - Is this paper relevant to my current work?
  - Should I read it in depth?
  - What's the main contribution?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Second Pass: 1 hour">
  **Goal:** Grasp the paper's content and methodology
  
  **Read:**
  - Introduction and related work carefully
  - Main sections, skipping detailed proofs
  - Experimental setup and results
  
  **Take Notes:**
  - Key ideas and techniques
  - Novel contributions vs. incremental improvements
  - Experimental design choices
  
  **Mark:**
  - References to read next
  - Unclear sections for deeper study
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Third Pass: 3-5 hours">
  **Goal:** Deeply understand and potentially reproduce
  
  **Do:**
  - Read every section in detail
  - Work through mathematical derivations
  - Question every assumption
  - Identify implicit details
  
  **Try:**
  - Reproduce key experiments
  - Implement core algorithms
  - Extend or modify the approach
</Accordion>

## Foundational Papers by Topic

### Transformer Architecture

<Accordion title="Attention Is All You Need (Vaswani et al., 2017)">
  **Citation:** Vaswani, A., Shazeer, N., Parmar, N., et al. (2017). Attention is all you need. *NeurIPS*.
  
  **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762
  
  **Core Contribution:**
  - Introduced the transformer architecture
  - Multi-head self-attention mechanism
  - Positional encodings
  - Eliminated recurrence and convolutions
  
  **Key Sections:**
  - Section 3.2: Multi-head attention
  - Section 3.5: Positional encoding
  - Section 5: Training details and results
  
  **Implementation Notes:**
  - Scaled dot-product attention: $\text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$
  - Layer normalization applied before sublayers (Post-LN vs Pre-LN)
  - Sinusoidal vs. learned positional embeddings
  
  **Week Alignment:** Weeks 4 (Attention Mechanics), 5 (Transformer Architecture)
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers (Devlin et al., 2018)">
  **Citation:** Devlin, J., Chang, M. W., Lee, K., & Toutanova, K. (2018). BERT: Pre-training of deep bidirectional transformers for language understanding. *arXiv preprint*.
  
  **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.04805
  
  **Core Contribution:**
  - Bidirectional pretraining with masked language modeling
  - Next sentence prediction task
  - Transfer learning via fine-tuning
  
  **Key Insights:**
  - Masked tokens force bidirectional context
  - Pre-training + fine-tuning paradigm
  - Importance of large-scale pretraining data
  
  **Week Alignment:** Week 6 (Training Objectives)
</Accordion>

### Scaling and Training

<Accordion title="Language Models are Few-Shot Learners (GPT-3) (Brown et al., 2020)">
  **Citation:** Brown, T. B., Mann, B., Ryder, N., et al. (2020). Language models are few-shot learners. *NeurIPS*.
  
  **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14165
  
  **Core Contribution:**
  - 175B parameter autoregressive language model
  - In-context learning without gradient updates
  - Scaling laws for model performance
  
  **Key Findings:**
  - Performance improves log-linearly with scale
  - Few-shot learning emerges at scale
  - Task-agnostic pretraining enables task-specific prompting
  
  **Important Figures:**
  - Figure 1.2: Scaling laws across model sizes
  - Figure 3.1: In-context learning curves
  
  **Week Alignment:** Weeks 8 (Scaling Laws), 10 (Evaluation)
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Training Compute-Optimal LLMs (Chinchilla) (Hoffmann et al., 2022)">
  **Citation:** Hoffmann, J., Borgeaud, S., Mensch, A., et al. (2022). Training compute-optimal large language models. *arXiv preprint*.
  
  **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.15556
  
  **Core Contribution:**
  - Optimal scaling: model size and training tokens should scale equally
  - Previous models were undertrained
  - Chinchilla (70B) outperforms Gopher (280B) with same compute
  
  **Key Equations:**
  - $N_{\text{optimal}} \propto C^{0.5}$ (model parameters)
  - $D_{\text{optimal}} \propto C^{0.5}$ (training tokens)
  
  **Implications:**
  - Rethinking compute allocation
  - More data, smaller models for fixed compute
  
  **Week Alignment:** Week 8 (Scaling Laws)
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models (Kaplan et al., 2020)">
  **Citation:** Kaplan, J., McCandlish, S., Henighan, T., et al. (2020). Scaling laws for neural language models. *arXiv preprint*.
  
  **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.08361
  
  **Core Contribution:**
  - Power-law relationships for loss vs. model size, dataset size, compute
  - Empirical scaling law coefficients
  - Prediction of performance from resource allocation
  
  **Key Insights:**
  - Loss scales as $L(N) \sim N^{-0.076}$ (model size)
  - Loss scales as $L(D) \sim D^{-0.095}$ (dataset size)
  - Compute-optimal model size grows with compute budget
  
  **Week Alignment:** Week 8 (Scaling Laws)
</Accordion>

### Alignment and Safety

<Accordion title="Training language models to follow instructions (InstructGPT) (Ouyang et al., 2022)">
  **Citation:** Ouyang, L., Wu, J., Jiang, X., et al. (2022). Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback. *NeurIPS*.
  
  **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.02155
  
  **Core Contribution:**
  - Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)
  - Three-stage training: SFT → Reward modeling → PPO fine-tuning
  - Improved helpfulness and reduced harmful outputs
  
  **Key Methodology:**
  1. Supervised fine-tuning on demonstrations
  2. Train reward model from human comparisons
  3. Optimize policy with PPO against reward model
  
  **Week Alignment:** Week 11 (Alignment and Safety)
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback (Bai et al., 2022)">
  **Citation:** Bai, Y., Kadavath, S., Kundu, S., et al. (2022). Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI feedback. *arXiv preprint*.
  
  **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.08073
  
  **Core Contribution:**
  - Self-improvement via AI-generated critiques and revisions
  - Reducing reliance on human feedback for harmlessness
  - Constitution: set of principles guiding model behavior
  
  **Key Stages:**
  1. Supervised stage: Model critiques and revises its own responses
  2. RL stage: AI-generated preference labels for RLAIF
  
  **Week Alignment:** Week 11 (Alignment and Safety)
</Accordion>

### Interpretability

<Accordion title="A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits (Elhage et al., 2021)">
  **Citation:** Elhage, N., Nanda, N., Olsson, C., et al. (2021). A mathematical framework for transformer circuits. *Transformer Circuits Thread*.
  
  **URL:** https://transformer-circuits.pub/2021/framework/index.html
  
  **Core Contribution:**
  - Residual stream as central communication channel
  - Attention heads as specialized circuits
  - Layer-by-layer information flow analysis
  
  **Key Concepts:**
  - QK and OV circuits
  - Induction heads for in-context learning
  - Compositional structure of attention patterns
  
  **Week Alignment:** Week 12 (Interpretability and Mechanistic Understanding)
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="In-context Learning and Induction Heads (Olsson et al., 2022)">
  **Citation:** Olsson, C., Elhage, N., Nanda, N., et al. (2022). In-context learning and induction heads. *arXiv preprint*.
  
  **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.11895
  
  **Core Contribution:**
  - Induction heads emerge during training and enable in-context learning
  - Phase change in loss coincides with induction head formation
  - Mechanistic explanation for few-shot learning
  
  **Key Experiments:**
  - Ablation studies showing induction heads are necessary
  - Training dynamics showing sudden capability emergence
  
  **Week Alignment:** Week 12 (Interpretability)
</Accordion>

### Multimodal

<Accordion title="An Image is Worth 16x16 Words (Vision Transformer) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020)">
  **Citation:** Dosovitskiy, A., Beyer, L., Kolesnikov, A., et al. (2020). An image is worth 16x16 words: Transformers for image recognition at scale. *ICLR*.
  
  **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.11929
  
  **Core Contribution:**
  - Pure transformer architecture for image classification
  - Image patches as sequence tokens
  - Competitive with CNNs when pretrained at scale
  
  **Key Design:**
  - Patch embedding: split image into 16×16 patches
  - Learnable position embeddings
  - Classification token for image-level prediction
  
  **Week Alignment:** Week 13 (Computer Vision Transformers)
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Learning Transferable Visual Models From Natural Language Supervision (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021)">
  **Citation:** Radford, A., Kim, J. W., Hallacy, C., et al. (2021). Learning transferable visual models from natural language supervision. *ICML*.
  
  **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.00020
  
  **Core Contribution:**
  - Contrastive learning on 400M image-text pairs
  - Zero-shot transfer to downstream vision tasks
  - Alignment between vision and language representations
  
  **Key Methodology:**
  - Dual encoder: image encoder + text encoder
  - Contrastive loss: maximize similarity of correct pairs
  - Natural language as supervision signal
  
  **Week Alignment:** Week 15 (Multimodal Foundations)
</Accordion>

### Organizing Your Reading

1. **Priority Tiers:**
   - Must-read: Foundational papers for current week
   - Should-read: Supporting papers and extensions
   - Nice-to-read: Adjacent topics and future directions

2. **Note-Taking Template:**
   - One-sentence summary
   - Key contributions (3-5 bullets)
   - Methodology overview
   - Results and limitations
   - Connections to other work
   - Implementation notes

3. **Implementation Tracking:**
   - Code repositories implementing the paper
   - Your own implementation attempts
   - Differences between paper and code
   - Reproduction results
